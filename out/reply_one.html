The two interpersonal communication skills that I’m working on are assertive self-expression, and being able to adapt my messages to account for the unique perspective of the other person. 

 

I took the chance to practice my assertive self-expression skill with my mom. She asked me to help her organize her house and get her papers in order; however, it was not a good day for me, and I did not think that I would do the best I could. I used “I” statements to get my message across, and informed her that I would be of more help another day. She understood this, and we agreed upon another time. This allowed me to not only be more open with myself, but with my family. I made sure to be honest with myself about how I was feeling, and to be more assertive with people, so I wouldn't end up troubling the other person and myself. I practiced adapting my messages while I was at work on Sunday. A regular came in and sparked up a conversation with me. I had not really spoken a ton with him before, so I took this opportunity to do so. When I was speaking with him, I inquired about his past and worked from the information I got. This allowed me to hone in my abilities on adapting my messages, and I got to know more about him, which resulted in a better and deeper conversation. Pretty much every time I practiced my skills, they were a success. Of course, some days were better than others, but overall, there wasn’t really a day that was highly unsuccessful. Every day I am able to get better at working on my interpersonal communication, and each time I can learn from my conversations, and what I did right versus what I need to improve on. 

 

I, personally, don’t think my non-verbals give too much away when I am communicating. I am pretty good at keeping myself in check with my body language, and displaying that I am engaged in the conversation. I will say though, that a lot of the time when I am conversing with others, I tend to cross my arms. This is not because I am annoyed or disinterested, it is just a comfortable position for me, and one I resort to. However, this could be received negatively by the person I am having a conversation with, and make me come across as rude. I could definitely work on this more, so my non-verbals don’t say one thing, when I am actually feeling the opposite. One example of this was when I was speaking with my friend, I crossed my arms, but soon after I noticed a shift in his attitude. I realized that he probably got the impression that I was annoyed, angry, not interested in the conversation, or somewhere in between. I immediately changed my body language to show that I was in fact engrossed in the conversation.
